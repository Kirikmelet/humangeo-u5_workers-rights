Origins of Labor Unions and Workers' Rights

The history of Labor Unions have been marred with violence and struggle. But that doesn't mean they haven't done good for us.

A Long and Proud History

Within Britain existed a law called 'The Ordinance of Labourers' enacted in the Kingdom of England, 1349. This basically outlawed collective bargaining in unity. the Royal government would later specifically surpress labor unions with the 18th/19th century "Combination Acts", of which banned the organization of labor unions and forced them underground. Only was it due to public pressure and strikes that the government reallow the existance of labor unions. The idea of trade unions later spread among the industrialized or industrializing western powers. The gains of unions will be discussed in chapter 3.

Spread and History of the Labor Unions in the United States

Labor Unions later spread from it's European Core and started to pop up in countries like our dear United States. One notable court case in the US in regards to early labor unions was held in March 1842 (Commonwealth vs. Hunt). Where the court rules that collective was in of itself illegal but that unions were legal. This lead to a rise in labor unions across the US.
A couple famed and well-known unions were:

  • the Knights of Labor
  • the American Federation of Labor
  • the Western Federation of Labor

Guess which still exists today.

Labor was at it's peak, and it's most violent during the early-mid 20th century. At this time, the bottom 40% owned a mighty less than 0% of the nation's wealth. (in comparison of the wealthiest 1% owning a meager portion of 51% of the national wealth) Labor strikes often turned violent and deadly, culminating in armed firefights between striking workers and local/state authorities. The bloodiest portion of this labor period will be explored in chapter 2. Needless to say, many Socialist, Communist, Anarchist, and including Progressive organizations/groups/unions sprung up. During the Great Depression of 1929(-1936), the power of the Labor Movement was at it's highest ever, and then-President Franklin Deleano Roosevelt was arguably the US' most pro-labor president. Under his presidency several things were either signed into law or were suggested.
These laws include:

  • the New Deal
  • the National Labor Relations Act of 1935
  • the National Industrial Recovery Act of 1933
  • the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938
  • the Securities Act of 1933
  • the Social Security Act of 1935
  • the Davis–Bacon Act of 1931
  • the Walsh–Healey Public Contracts Act of 1936
  • the Securities Exchange Act of 1934

However, following the post-war era and into the cold war, the (2nd) Red Scare and general apathy among the populus, caused some labor laws to be rolled back.
