A Chicago Headline!

In the proud and windy midwest city of Chicago, happened one of the first bloodiest worker's strike in America. The Pullman Strike of 1894 started because the Pullman Company laid off workers, lowered wages, but refused to give the workers' any semblance of Democracy, cut excessive gas and water rates, and had a strong dictatorial presence over the workers. It did not help by the fact that the workers lived in Company Towns, and so was in all other means controlled and managed by the company.

The workers, led by Eugene Debs (later co-founder of the Socialist Party of America) founded the American Railway Union, and went on a wildcat strike (a state unauthorized/unrecognized strike). The union called for a boycott on all trains that had Pullman cars, which affected most rail west of Detroit, and involved around 250,000 workers in 27 states. The strike and riots was so large that other unions (especially the AFL) opposed it. Sabotage, riots, and burnings racked up a debt of $80 million dollars worth of damage, and 30 people were killed. The Federal Government, filed an injuction against the strikers, but was ultimately ignored, leading to thousands of US Marshals and 12,000 United States Army infantry to quell the strike in Chicago. this caused violence to escalate, and on July 7th, 1894, after scuffles broke out between US infantrymen and strikers, the National Guard shot into the crowd, and 4-30 people were found dead.

Public Opinion by that point had long turned sour, and Eugene Debs was sent to prison for 6 months. However, this strike resulted in the establishment of the American Labor Day on September 1st, and forced Pullman to handover it's town to the City of Chicago, it is now a Chicago Landmark District.

The Cold War in Colorado

The longest in my project :Q

The Colorado Labor Wars of 1903-1904 was a series of violent strikes between the Western Federation of Miners (WFM) and "Employer Associations" and the Colorado State Government. Before this, the Cripple Creek strike of 1894 was one of the first confrontations between the WFM and the mine owners. The mine owners raised the work hours without raising pay, causing a strike to break out, the owners in result brought in 1,200 strike breakers. Both sides were well armed and prepared for a confrontation. However, the Governer of Colorado, Davis Waite, convinced both sides in favor of the union workers.

In 1903, conservative elements of Coloradan society and corporate interests elected a pro-business Republican. The newly inaugurated Governer James Peabody promised to make Colorado "Safe for Investments.". In coinciding with earlier, employers themselves started their own associations or unions. Pinkerton spies commonly monitored union members on behalf of the companies as well. In comparison, frustrations of the WFM and the state government led to the WFM in adopting (Revolutionary) Socialism.

The fruits for bloodshed started when the Coloradan Lesgislature refused to acknowledge to vote on an 8-hour work day, despite a state referendum in which 72% of the population voted in favor of it, and a constitutional amendment for the Coloradan Supreme Court to be satisfied. A strike in Idaho Springs in 1903 was started to push for an 8-hour work day. 2 months into the strike, the striking miners attempted to blow up a powerhouse in one of the strike breaker's (commonly referred to as scabs) housing units. However, one of the strikers died in a pre-mature blast. Union officials were arrested that night under suspicions. Interestingly enough, pro-employer people under the "Citizen's Protection League" removed the union members and exiled them from town. A strike at the Durango Smelter failed due to the hirement of scabs. Another incident occured when a Pinkerton spy caused 42 miners to be fired for simply joining a union, a strike was called in and the state government deployed the Colorado Nation Guard despite protests and objections by Colorado-City City-Officials. The National Guard dispersed union pickets and searched their homes. Various other strikes occured at this era but weren't as severe. The most severe strike however, occured in August of 1903, when the WFM in attempt to force mills to the negotiation table forcibly started strikes in associated mines, unfortunately, the strike was done counter to democratic norms and was opposed by up to 90% of WFM miners. The reason for the WFM's desition was that they followed "Industrial Unionism", where all union members in an Industry collectivize in solidarity with each other. A sort of cold war existed between WFM influenced areas and Cripple Creek Mine Owner's Association (CCMOA) influenced areas. Merchants by then refused to do business there. The sheriff of Cripple Creek was a WFM member, and deputized guards at mines although faced dismay by the mine owners.

After an investigation by the Governer of Colorado, he intervened in support of the CCMOA and deployed 1000 National Guardsmen. It was protested by the populus. The general in charge of the battalion or so was an avowed de-facto miltarist, with qoutes such as "To hell with the constitution, we aren't going by the constitution.", and "Habeas corpus be damned, we'll give 'em post mortems." The National Guard began suppresing and arresting anyone with seemingly pro-union sympathies. Any journalist in support of the strikers were temporarily detained. When several arrested union members were brought to court, the National Guard escorted them armed into the courtroom, and remained armed and in formation whilst inside the chamber. Some soldiers outside setup a machine gun post and some took up sniper duties. In face of intimidation, an attorney for the prisoners left in protest, but the Judge ruled in favor of the prisoners while commenting, "I trust that there will never again be such an unseemly and unnecessary intrusion of armed soldiers in the halls and about the entrances of American Courts of Justice. They are intrusions that can only tend to bring this court into contempt, and make doubtful the boasts of that liberty that is the keynote of American Government.". In spite of the court ruling, the National Guard refused to comply until the Governer ordered them to do so. Mine owners hired scabs with them under the impression that they were permanent work, not as a replacement in strikes. Those who were hired were practically forced to work despite the strikes. One notable example was when a scab miner named Emil Peterson immediately left when he found out that the workers at the mine were striking, when he ran an officer shot at him, the officer was never arrested despite a warrant.

A WFM member was bribed by Pinkertons to pull the spikes of a traintrack where Victor's Mine Union leaders and men were supposed to travel on that day. The offending member, McKinney, released 2 confessions, the first one implicated Union leaders and the WFm, while his 2nd confession contradicts the first by saying that he was payed a pardon, immunity, $1000, and the abiltity for him and his wife to travel the world by lying who pulled them, as he himself didn't know who. McKinney ultimately stuck to his first confession and said that his 2nd was false. He was granted unusual privileges as he was allowed to visit his wife. One of the Pinkertons admitted to trying to get WFM members to pull the spike, in order to create the conditions to condemn the WFM. The jury found the WFM members non-guiilty. The Pinkertons were never arrested.

A strike occured in September 1903, where WFM members, armed, struck for an 8-hour workday. The Governer asked for US Army infrantrymen, but President Roosevelt refused. He later settled for 500 Colorado National Guardsmen. Deputity Sheriffs attempted to disarm the union members, but they refused, in response, the deputies started arresting any striking members for vagrancy. The charges would later be dropped, and released from jails a mere 71 miles only.

A WFM member, Harry Orchard, confessed to causing an explosion at Vindicator Mine, despite the areas being heavily fotified and guard by the National Guard. The explosion killed 2 management employees of the mine. Both sides (WFM and CCMOA) accused each other of the bomb, the National Guard increased it's harrassment of union members, and started arresting children who annoyed the soldiers, and and the governer delcared martial law. The Bill of Rights was suspended, prisoners who won Habeas Corpus cases were rearrested, people who refused to give up arms faced the risk of getting shot, and freedoms of assembly and speech was surpressed. It was alleged that the explosion was caused by the mine owners to prolong the National Guard's deployment. Harray Orchard, also confessed to pulling the spikes (as mentioned 2 paragraphs ago), while also implicating WFM leadersip, this is in contradiction with the jury and evidence involved in the case.

The final blow to the WFM occured on June 6th, 1904, when an explosion happened at the Independence Train Depot, taking 13 with it. The CCMOA and allied groups vowed to forcibly remove all non-supportive officials. They forced the sheriff to resign immediately, or face a lynching. CCMOA later held a rally across WFM headquarters, this caused a firefight to occur between pro-CCMOA miltias (with National Guard support), and WFM miners. All mine management employees were deuptized the night of the explosion, and hunted all union mumbers they could find. The found and hunted were barred from food, until the Women's Auxillary were allowed to. Those arrested would be deported across state lines via kangaroo courts.

The 10 Day War in Slovenia Colorado

The Colorado Coalfield War of 1913-1914, infamous for being the US's most bloodiest labor strike ever. In 1903, the Colorado Fuel and Iron Company (CF&I) was taken over by a group supported by John D. Rockefeller. Under his tenure, working conditions dropped dramtically, and death rates were twice the average there. And despite Federal concerns, nothing ever changed at the mines. Labor spying was also commonplace, and only increased after the 1903-1904 strikes. Because CF&I didn't comply with new labor regulations, the workers went on strike. Forces allied with CF&I hired private detectives, and then-Governer (of Colorado) Elis Ammons deployed the National Guard. The detectives then murdered a United Mine Workers of America (UMWA) organizer 5 weeks prior to the strikes. Oddly enough, the CF&I had an armored car named "Death Special", which was equipped with around 9 machine guns and was to fire into the miners' colonies. Both sides then started an arms race.

When the first strike occured, a peak of 20,000 went on strike and evicted from their homes, they would later be housed in tents, although some came late, and those who didn't have tents had to live under their furniture However, violence would start on September 24, 1913, in where a generally disliked CF&I marshal was ambushed and killed. CF&I-aligned militias shot from a route that connected Front Range and got 0 casualties. On October 9, 1913, a string miner-turned-rancher was shot dead by CF&I guards on patrol. This caused an arms race. While escorting a family of a scab, deputies fired into a crowd of strikers, killing 3 foreign miners. Greek miners would later assemble to ambush a military train due to them fearing the National Guard. Soon later, UMWA strikers would start harassing, ambushing, and attacking scabs and CF&I-aligned forces. On January 11 of 1914, a famed labor activist, Mother Jones, was arrested and detained at a hospital for 9 months, strikers tried and failed to free her. On January 14, 1914, an unexploded bomb was found in the National Guards' camp. Several UMWA members were arrested. Thanks to military intimidation and Greek union leaders (AN: really Wikipedia?), the strikes became peaceful. But National Guard officers were concered that one of their men was directly causing confrontation with the UMWA strikers. Lt. Linderfelt, was accused of hitting a striker for failing to disclose the location of a child. The National Guard denied the claim, but raised concerns over how the Lieutenant talked to the boy, in where he said, "I am Jesus Christ, and my men on horses are Jesus Christs, and we must be obeyed.". A scab was found dead on March 8, 1914, at a time when a congresional comittee was on tour the the body's general vicinity. The National Guard later burned down a striker camp and accidently killed 2 newborn infants.

On April 20, 1914, tensions peaked when Louis Tikos, the leader of the strikers, was summoned by the National guard, and both the UMWA and the National Guard took entrenchments. The first shots rang out, and Tikas was found shot in the back of the head. A twelve-year old was shot in the head and had his brain blown out, an and National Guard private was shot in the neck. 18 UMWA casaulties were suffered. The UMWA would declare war, and violent conflicts occured between pro-Union forces and pro-CF&I forces. There are too many conflicts to be mentioned here, but fighting lasted until the Union ran out of funds in 1914. The Union agreed to an unconditional surrender with the US Federal Government serving as an intermediately.

Lt. Linderfelt and Major Patrick Hamrock were charged in independent court-martials assault and suppresion of the strikes. Several UMWA members had their convictions overturned, and the UMWA bought a 40-acre large land to build a monument commerating the strikes.
